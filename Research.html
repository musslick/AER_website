Autonomous Empirical Research Group

Mission

Our research group strives to enhance and accelerate scientific discovery by automating each step of the empirical research process, from constructing a scientific hypothesis to conducting novel experiments. To this end, we devise an open-source programming language for autonomous empirical research. We seek to co-create and share this system with empirical scientists across disciplines who desire to advance their research without sacrificing scientific standards, such as reproducibility and transparency. Finally, we seek to accelerate open science efforts by developing methods for the automated documentation and dissemination of steps taken in the empirical research process.

Approach

Autonomous Empirical Research (AER) consists of two artificial agents that compete and together implement a form of adversarial curiosity: (a) an autonomous theorist whose objective is to construct computational models from existing data by linking experiment conditions to dependent measures, and (b) an autonomous experimentalist whose objective is to invalidate the theorist by deriving predictions from its models and designing novel experiments to collect unexplained data.

To implement AER, we seek to automate different steps in the empirical research process, such as devising a theory from collected data, designing an experiment to test predictions of a theory, and running an experiment to collect new data. In addition, we seek to develop methods for automating the documentation and dissemination of steps taken in the empirical research process.

Automating the Construction of Scientific Models

Most theories of human behavior and brain function can be expressed in terms of quantitative models that link experimental parameters to observed outcomes. We seek to automate the process of devising quantitative models of human cognition and behavior in the form of small, interpretable computation graphs. A computation graph takes experimental parameters as input (e.g., the brightness of a visual stimulus). It transforms this input through a sequence of functions to produce observable dependent measures as output (e.g., the probability that a participant can detect the stimulus). The process of developing a computational model incurs two challenges: (1) identifying the architecture of the model, i.e., the composition of functions and parameters, and (2) tuning parameters of the model to fit behavioral data. We leverage a combination of techniques that emerged from the past years of machine learning to automate the construction and fitting of quantitative models to explain human behavior and brain function. 

Automating the Derivation of Novel Experiments

The primary goal of an experimentalist is to identify experiments that yield scientific merit. An experiment carries scientific merit if it yields novel data that existing scientific models fail to explain. Here, we seek to leverage active learning–a machine learning framework for identifying new data points to inform the improvement of an existing model–to identify experiment conditions that existing computational models may fail to explain. In addition, we extend a newly developed programming language for automating experimental design ( SweetPea ) to devise a pipeline for embedding novel experiment conditions into a counterbalanced sequence of experimental trials.

Automating Open Science

One of the most significant obstacles of open science is the time and effort required to standardize and document each step in the empirical research process, such as the generation of experimental designs or computational models. We seek to address this challenge by developing methods for the automated documentation of (autonomously conducted) experiments. These methods build on community-supported and machine-readable standards for expressing quantitative models of brain function and behavior (e.g., the Model Description Format) and corresponding data sets (e.g., the Brain Imaging Data Structure). Our tools are designed to automate the translation of those data sets into the English language and automate uploads thereof to the Open Science Framework.
