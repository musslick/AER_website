Autonomous Empirical Research Group

Mission

Our research group is dedicated to facilitating scientific discovery by automating the multiple stages of the empirical research process, encompassing the formulation of scientific hypotheses to the implementation of follow-up experiments. To achieve this goal, we are currently engaged in the development of an open-source framework for autonomous empirical research , intended to enhance open science initiatives through the automation, documentation and communication of steps in the empirical research process. Our group actively collaborates with researchers across different domains, with the shared objective of creating and sharing tools that uphold rigorous scientific standards of reproducibility and transparency, thereby promoting scientific progress.

Paradigm

Autonomous Empirical Research (AER)  is a paradigm that involves the dynamic interplay between two artificial agents. The first agent, a theorist, is primarily responsible for constructing computational models through the linkage of experimental conditions to dependent measures, using existing data as a basis. The second agent, an experimentalist, is tasked with designing follow-up experiments that can refine and validate the models generated by the theorist. Together, these agents enable a closed-loop scientific discovery process. In addition, AER interfaces with platforms for automated data collection, such as Prolific or Amazon Mechanical Turk, which enable the efficient acquisition of behavioral data from human participants.  

To enable closed-loop empirical research, we develop tools for automating different steps in the empirical research process, such as devising a theory from collected data, designing an experiment to test predictions of a theory, and running an experiment to collect new data. In addition, we seek to develop methods for automating the documentation and dissemination of steps taken in the empirical research process.

Automating the Construction of Scientific Models

Scientific models are often expressed in the form of interpretable equations or programs that establish a linkage between experimental parameters (independent variables) and observable outcomes (dependent variables). Constructing scientific models in a mathematical or computational form poses two main challenges: (1) determining the architecture of the model, which involves identifying the composition and sequence of operations, and (2) identifying the parameters of the model that can best fit the available data. To address these challenges, we rely on a combination of techniques derived from the fields of machine learning and automated scientific discovery. These models take the role of a theorist in the AER paradigm. Our goal is to automate the construction and fitting of scientific models, thus enabling researchers to more efficiently and effectively uncover meaningful insights into the phenomena they study.

Automating the Derivation of Novel Experiments

The primary goal of an experimentalist is to identify experiments that yield scientific merit. Our framework offers a multitude of experimental strategies for identifying new data points, e.g., by searching for experiment conditions that an existing scientific models fails to explain, or by looking for novel conditions. Automating the Collection of Data

Scientific data collection is often both expensive and time-consuming. To address these bottlenecks, we develop tools for automating the process of collecting experimental data (e.g., behavioral data obtained from crowd-sourced online experiments), and for augmenting empirical data that is not feasible to collect in an automated manner (e.g., EEG data).

Automating Open Science

One of the most significant obstacles of open science is the time and effort required to standardize and document each step in the empirical research process, such as the generation of experimental designs or computational models. We seek to address this challenge by developing methods for the automated documentation of (automated) experiments. These methods build on community-supported and machine-readable standards for expressing scientific models in ther respective domain. Our tools are designed to automate the translation of those data sets into the English language and facilitate uploads thereof to the Open Science Framework (OSF).
